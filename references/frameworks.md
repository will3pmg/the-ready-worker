## 1. The core worldview

The Ready's argument, distilled:

- The dominant way of organizing work today (the "Legacy OS") was built ~100 years ago by Frederick Taylor and his contemporaries for industrial-era factories. It assumes thinking and doing should be separated, that workers need to be controlled, and that one best way exists for every task.
- That OS worked for a while. It no longer matches the world we live in. Productivity growth has stalled. Corporate longevity is collapsing. Most workers are disengaged.
- The problem isn't "the leaders" or "the people." The problem is the operating system itself — the implicit assumptions, principles, practices, and policies that shape how work happens.
- A new OS is emerging in pockets — Buurtzorg, Morning Star, Haier, Handelsbanken, FAVI, W.L. Gore, and dozens more. It's grounded in two ideas: **people-positive** (humans are worthy of trust and autonomy) and **complexity-conscious** (organizations are complex systems, not machines).
- You don't change an OS by writing values on posters. You change it by running small, safe-to-try experiments at the team level, learning what works, and letting it spread.

## 2. The 12-dimension OS Canvas

Every team and organization runs on twelve interconnected dimensions. When a tension surfaces, locating it in the right dimension(s) often reveals what's really going on. People rarely identify the dimension correctly on the first pass.

### Purpose
*How we orient and steer; the reason for being.*
- What is our reason for being?
- What is meaningful about our work?
- How does our purpose help us make decisions?

### Authority
*How we share power and make decisions.*
- Who can tell others what to do?
- How do we make important decisions?
- What is safe to try? What is not?

### Structure
*How we organize and team.*
- What is centralized? What is decentralized?
- Within teams, how do we approach roles and accountabilities?
- How does our structure learn or change over time?

### Strategy
*How we plan and prioritize.*
- What are the critical factors that will mean the difference between success and failure?
- How do we develop, refine, and refresh our strategy?
- How do we use strategy to filter and steer day-to-day?

### Resources
*How we invest our time and money.*
- How do we allocate funds, effort, space, and other assets?
- How do strategy and planning influence resource allocation?
- How does our approach enable us to respond to emergent events?

### Innovation
*How we learn and evolve.*
- Who participates in innovation? Who has the right to innovate?
- What is the role of failure and learning?
- How do we balance the short term and the long term?

### Workflow
*How we divide and do the work.*
- What is the relationship between our workflow and our structure?
- How do we maintain visibility across all our projects?
- How are projects initiated, canceled, or completed?

### Meetings
*How we convene and coordinate.*
- Does each meeting have a clear purpose and structure?
- How are meetings facilitated and documented?
- How do we improve or eliminate meetings that no longer serve us?

### Information
*How we share and use data.*
- What information is shared freely?
- What information is contained or controlled?
- What tools, systems, or forums support storing and sharing?

### Membership
*How we define and cultivate relationships.*
- How is membership gained? Relinquished? Revoked?
- What do members expect of one another?
- How do members move between teams and other boundaries?

### Mastery
*How we grow and mature.*
- What is our approach to learning and development?
- How do we give and receive feedback?
- How does competence influence the roles we inhabit?

### Compensation
*How we pay and provide.*
- What is our approach to compensation?
- What mechanisms reduce bias in compensation?
- How are changes in compensation triggered and conducted?

### Common misdiagnoses
- "We have too many meetings" → often actually a Workflow or Authority problem (people pulled into meetings because they can't decide async or alone)
- "We have a communication problem" → often actually an Information or Structure problem
- "Our culture is broken" → almost always rooted in specific dimensions; "culture" is the read-only output, not a thing you can fix directly
- "We're not innovative" → usually Authority + Resources + Workflow (people don't have safe-to-try permission, time, or paths)
- "Our team isn't aligned" → often Purpose or Strategy, sometimes Information

## 3. Complicated vs. complex

Two distinct kinds of systems, often confused.

**Complicated systems** are causal and predictable:
- A watch, a car engine, a FedEx delivery
- May require expertise to understand or fix
- Have right answers
- Can be "solved"
- Best approach: analyze, then act

**Complex systems** are dispositional and emergent:
- Weather, traffic, a six-year-old, a team, a culture, a market
- Surprise you regularly
- No right answers, only better/worse moves
- Can only be "managed" through interaction
- Best approach: act and observe, then act again

Most workplace problems are complex. The classic mistake is applying complicated-system thinking (big plan, perfect rollout, comprehensive analysis) to a complex problem. Symptoms: long planning cycles, frustration when the plan doesn't survive contact with reality, blame when things don't go as designed.

When the user describes a situation, listen for whether they're treating a complex thing as complicated. Common tells:
- "Once we have the full picture, we can..." (you'll never have the full picture)
- "We just need to make sure everyone..." (you can't centrally control a complex system)
- "If we get this right the first time..." (you won't; design for iteration)

## 4. The change loop

The core method for shaping how a team works. Repeats continuously.

### Step 1: Surface the tension
"What's stopping you from doing the best work of your life?" is the canonical opening question. Other variants:
- What's slowing you down?
- What's frustrating?
- What feels stupid?
- What would you change if you could?

Tensions should be specific and grounded. Not "we have a culture problem" — but "I can't get a decision out of my manager on X" or "every Tuesday meeting runs over and we never decide anything."

### Step 2: Explore candidate practices
What's a different way to approach this? Sources:
- How do other teams handle this?
- How do other companies handle this? (Buurtzorg, Morning Star, Haier, Handelsbanken, FAVI, etc.)
- What's the opposite of what we're doing now?
- What would we do if we were starting fresh?

Don't optimize prematurely. List a few candidates.

### Step 3: Design a safe-to-try experiment
The key question: is the learning worth the squeeze? Define:
- What exactly we're going to try
- Who's doing it
- How long we'll run it (usually 2-8 weeks)
- How we'll know if it helped, hurt, or changed nothing
- What we'll do at the end (keep, kill, modify)

### Step 4: Run and reflect
Hold a small retrospective. Did it help? Did it hurt? What surprised us? What do we want to try next?

### What spreads, spreads
If a practice serves a team, they'll tell other teams. If the org has enough trust and autonomy, other teams will adopt it. Sometimes leadership formalizes what's already working. This is how change actually scales — bottom-up, evidence-based, not via mandate.

## 5. Authority

The most contested and consequential dimension. Three core concepts:

### The waterline
A metaphor from W.L. Gore. If you're sailing a boat, a hole above the waterline can be patched later. A hole below the waterline sinks the ship.

- **Above the waterline** = safe-to-try, recoverable, low-stakes → just decide
- **Below the waterline** = irreversible, high-stakes, affects the whole system → don't decide alone

The waterline isn't fixed. It varies by:
- Company size and stage (a startup's waterline is higher than an enterprise's)
- The decider's role and experience
- Current conditions (cash position, market state, etc.)

Many people wait for permission on decisions that are clearly above their waterline. Naming "this is above your waterline" can unstick them.

### The advice process
Before making a meaningful decision, seek advice from:
1. People with relevant **expertise**
2. People who will be **affected**

You still decide. But you decide informed. This is *not* consensus — consensus paralyzes. It's not delegation — you still own the call. It's not a vote. It's just: get the input that will make your call better.

Used at Morning Star, AES, Buurtzorg, The Ready, and others.

### Integrative decisions
For decisions that affect multiple roles or domains (e.g., compensation policy, big strategic pivots), require **consent** from multiple roles before acting. Consent ≠ agreement. Consent = "I don't see a reason this would harm us." It's a lower bar than agreement but a higher bar than one person deciding.

### Reserved decisions
A small set of decisions reserved for specific roles — e.g., the captain of the USS Santa Fe reserved one decision (firing a nuclear weapon). Everything else was distributed.

### Practical reframe
When someone is stuck on "should I just decide or should I get approval," the cleaner questions are:
- Is this above or below my waterline?
- Who has expertise I should pull in?
- Who will be affected and deserves a heads-up or input?
- Is this an integrative decision that needs consent from multiple roles?

## 6. Organizational debt

A concept by analogy to financial and technical debt. Org debt = rules, policies, structures, and processes that no longer serve us but persist because no one refactored them.

The FAVI gloves story: workers needed manager approval to replace worn-out gloves. The process was created after a theft. The gloves cost €5. The downtime cost €5,000. Nobody updated the policy until a new CEO noticed it.

Common patterns:
- Approval workflows from a different scale of company
- Policies created in response to one bad incident, never revisited
- Meetings that exist because they used to be useful
- Reports that go to people who don't read them
- Tools and systems used because we already pay for them
- Roles that exist because we created them for someone who left

Spotting org debt:
- "Why do we do it this way?" → "Because we always have"
- "Who decided this?" → "I don't know, it predates me"
- "When was the last time we questioned this?" → "Never"
- "What problem does this solve?" → vague or stale answers

The fix is rarely "design a better policy." It's often "stop doing this and see what hurts."

## 7. People-positive vs. people-negative

Every org has implicit assumptions about people. The Ready argues most orgs default to people-negative without realizing it.

**People-negative assumptions:**
- People need to be incentivized to work
- People will slack off without oversight
- People can't be trusted with information
- People will make bad decisions without rules
- People need to be told what to do

**People-positive assumptions:**
- People are worthy of trust and respect
- People want autonomy, mastery, and purpose (Daniel Pink)
- People rise to expectations
- People will use judgment if given context
- People can shape their own work

Tells that an org is operating on people-negative assumptions:
- Time tracking, badge swipes, surveillance
- Approval workflows for small decisions
- Information control ("need to know")
- One-size-fits-all policies
- Heavy use of individual incentives

You can't argue someone into people-positive. You demonstrate it through small experiments that show people can be trusted with more.

## 8. Heuristics for participatory change

From the closing of Aaron Dignan's talks:

**Through them, not to them.** Change has to happen with participation. Not a PowerPoint written over the weekend and rolled out to thousands.

**Learn by doing.** Stop arguing about the right way to meet, decide, give feedback. Just try something and find out.

**Start small.** Big systems are addicted to scale. The thing that moves a $1B org 1% has to be a $10M idea. But change actually spreads through small experiments at the team level. Don't try to move the boulder.

**Start by stopping.** Subtract before you add. Pause a meeting series. Drop a policy. See what hurts. Often nothing does.

**Join the resistance.** When someone isn't adopting a change, don't write them off. Ask why. They usually have a real reason. The system may be set up against them. Their feedback is a gift.

## 9. Vocabulary glossary

- **OS / Operating System** — the implicit and explicit set of assumptions, principles, practices, and policies that shape how work happens
- **Legacy OS** — the Taylorist, industrial-era OS most orgs run on
- **Evolutionary Organization** — orgs that have moved beyond the Legacy OS (Buurtzorg, Morning Star, etc.)
- **Tension** — what's getting in the way of best work; the input to change
- **Practice** — a specific way of doing something (meeting, deciding, hiring, etc.)
- **Experiment** — a safe-to-try attempt to shift a practice
- **Safe to try** — the bar for action: not "is this right" but "is the learning worth the risk"
- **Waterline** — the threshold below which decisions need more deliberation
- **Advice process** — seeking input from expertise + affected before deciding
- **Integrative decision** — requires consent from multiple roles
- **Org debt** — accumulated rules and processes that no longer serve
- **Loop** — the tension → practice → experiment → reflect cycle
- **Roundabout vs. traffic light** — metaphor for trust+autonomy vs. control+compliance
- **Enabling constraints** — minimal rules that allow rich behavior (e.g., the two roundabout rules)
- **Sabotage manual** — the WWII OSS sabotage instructions, indistinguishable from modern corporate norms

## 10. Worked examples

### Example 1: "My boss is a micromanager"

Bad response: "Have you considered the OS Canvas's Authority dimension? You might want to..."

Better response:
"What's the specific thing they're doing that's getting in your way? And what's the smallest version of taking that back you could try this week — even just one decision you make without checking in?"

(This is the loop, applied conversationally. Tension → candidate practice → safe-to-try experiment. Without naming any of those things.)

### Example 2: "We have too many meetings"

Bad response: "Let me walk you through the 12 dimensions..."

Better response:
"How many of your meetings would you keep if no one was making you go? Pick one to stop attending for two weeks and see what happens. (And what is it about the meetings that's not working — the volume, the quality, or the fact that nothing gets decided in them?)"

(Start by stopping. Plus a diagnostic question: meetings problems are often Authority or Workflow problems wearing a Meetings costume.)

### Example 3: A founder asking "How do I scale culture as we grow from 30 to 100?"

Bad response: A list of culture-scaling tips.

Better response:
"Culture is read-only — it's the output of the OS, not something you scale directly. What are the practices that produce the culture you want now? Which of those will break first under 3x headcount? That's where the work is."

(Reframe the question. Push back on the culture-as-thing-you-engineer framing.)

### Example 4: An IC asking "Should I bring this up with my manager?"

Coaching first:
"What outcome are you hoping for? And what happens if you don't bring it up?"

Then, if useful:
"If you do bring it up, you've got a few flavors of move available: surface the tension and stop there (let them choose what to do with it), propose a specific small experiment, or just ask for advice without expecting them to solve it. Which fits this situation?"

(Advice process applied to upward communication.)

### Example 5: "We need to roll out a new performance review process"

Bad response: Help them design the rollout.

Better response:
"Through them, not to them. What if you piloted it with one team that opts in, ran it for a quarter, then let what works spread? Trying to install a new process across the whole org all at once is a 1955 move."

(Push back on the rollout framing. Reframe to participatory change.)
